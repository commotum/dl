In other words, any conditional statement of the form

can be expressed equivalently as

For example, consider the following conditional statement:

If you do not take your watch, then you will be late for the meeting.

Let's rewrite this statement using its logically equivalent form.

First, we denote the antecedent and consequent as follows:

  • You do not take your watch.

  • You will be late for the meeting.

Then, our initial statement can be written as which is logically equivalent to

The negation of is:

  • You take your watch.

Therefore, the given statement is logically equivalent to the following:

You take your watch, or you will be late for the meeting.

We can write this using more everyday language as follows:

Take your watch, or you will be late for the meeting.

Another way to write this is to append the word "either" to the beginning of the sentence, like so:

Either you take your watch, or you will be late for the meeting.

Using the word "either" helps clarify that the sentence structure presents two alternatives and is particularly useful when employing the disjunctive form in conversational or formal English. So, the phrase effectively conveys that one of the two stated conditions will hold true.
