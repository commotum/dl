Introduction
?

When conducting a survey or study, the goal is usually to gather information from a sample that accurately represents the entire population.

If the sample is chosen well, where every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected (achieved by randomly selecting units from the entire population), we call it an unbiased sample; we can then use the results to make reliable conclusions about the entire population.

However, if the method of selecting participants systematically favors certain individuals or groups, the result is a biased sample. Biased samples can give a distorted picture of reality and lead to incorrect conclusions.

Forms of Sample Bias
?

There are many ways sampling bias can sneak in, often without researchers even realizing it. Let’s look at some of the most common sources of bias:

  • Convenience Sampling

    This occurs when selecting participants who are easiest to reach, for example, by only surveying friends, classmates, or people nearby, rather than using a random process.

    Problem: The sample may not accurately represent the population, as it disproportionately favors individuals with similar circumstances to those of the researchers.

  • Voluntary Response Sampling

    This occurs when, instead of selecting from a population at random, participation in a survey is left to the discretion of individuals, such as in online polls or call-in surveys.

    Problem: People with strong opinions, often negative ones, are more likely to respond, leading to a skewed sample.

  • Undercoverage

    Sometimes, important groups are unintentionally excluded from the sampling frame. For instance, using a landline phone directory excludes households that do not have landlines.

    Problem: Entire segments of the population have zero chance of being selected.

  • Survivorship Bias

    This occurs when only "survivors" or cases that remain after some filtering are studied. For example, looking only at successful startups to find out what makes businesses succeed.

    Problem: Ignoring the failures hides valuable information and creates an overly optimistic picture.

  • Self-Selection within Groups

    Suppose a survey requires several different groups to be studied. Even if the initial groups are chosen randomly, bias can occur when participants within groups are allowed to opt in or out of certain parts of the study, for example, filling out an optional feedback survey.

    Problem: The subgroup that responds may not reflect the full diversity of the original sample.

Understanding these sources of bias helps us recognize when a sample might be misleading, and why unbiased sampling methods are essential for trustworthy conclusions.

A Worked Example
?

To see how these ideas apply in practice, let’s consider an example involving surveys at a concert.

Suppose a concert organizer wants to estimate the average amount of money attendees spend on merchandise during an event. They have the following ideas for collecting this information.

  • Method I: Surveying attendees seated in the upper balcony during the concert.

  • Method II: Asking only attendees who exit through the VIP lounge.

  • Method III: Randomly selecting attendees from the ticket sales database and contacting them about their merchandise purchases.

Let's determine which of these sampling methods is most likely to produce unbiased results.

Remember, an unbiased sample is one in which every member of the population has an equal chance of being included. To achieve this, we must select individual units at random from a complete list of the entire population.

Let's examine each sampling method in turn.

  • Method I: Surveying only attendees seated in the upper balcony is biased, because these individuals may have different spending habits than the average attendee.

  • Method II: Asking only people exiting through the VIP lounge is biased, since VIP guests may have above-average spending.

  • Method III: Randomly selecting attendees from the ticket sales database gives each attendee an equal chance of being chosen, so it can produce unbiased results.

Therefore, Method III is most likely to produce unbiased results.

Note: Methods I and II are examples of convenience sampling, where participants are chosen because they are easy to reach.

Example: Identifying Unbiased Sampling Methods
?

A hospital wants to estimate the average number of hours nurses work per week.

Which of the following sampling methods is most likely to produce unbiased results?

  1. Randomly selecting nurses from the hospital’s complete employee database.
  2. Randomly selecting nurses from the list of those assigned to the emergency department.
  3. Randomly selecting nurses from the list of those who attended a recent training workshop.
EXPLANATION

An unbiased sample is one in which every member of the population has an equal chance of being included. To achieve this, we must select individual units at random from a complete list of the entire population.

Let's examine each sampling method in turn.

  • Method I: Randomly selecting from the hospital’s complete employee database gives every nurse an equal chance of being chosen, so this method can produce unbiased results.

  • Method II: Randomly selecting from the emergency department list is biased because it excludes nurses working in other departments, leading to undercoverage. These nurses may have different work schedules.

  • Method III: Randomly selecting from recent training attendees is biased because it excludes nurses who did not attend that training, also leading to undercoverage. These nurses may have different workloads.

Therefore, the correct answer is "I only."

Note that Methods II and III are examples of undercoverage, where certain members of the population are systematically excluded from the sample.

Example: Identifying True Statements
?

A national library association wants to estimate how many books the average adult reads each year.

Which of the following sampling methods is most likely to produce unbiased results?

  1. Posting a survey on a popular book review website and asking visitors to respond.
  2. Asking people in the library café on a Saturday afternoon to complete a questionnaire.
  3. Randomly selecting adults from a national database of residents.
  4. Randomly choosing several groups of adults across the state, then asking for volunteers from each group to complete the survey.
EXPLANATION

An unbiased sample is one in which every member of the population (here, all adults in the country) has an equal chance of being included. To achieve this, we must select individuals at random from a complete list of the population.

Let's examine each method:

  • Method I: Posting on a book review site is voluntary response sampling, overrepresenting strong readers.

  • Method II: Asking people in the library café is convenience sampling, overrepresenting library users.

  • Method III: Randomly selecting from a national database of residents gives each registered resident an equal chance of being chosen, so it can produce unbiased results.

  • Method IV: Asking for volonteers from groups is self-selection within groups, it may attract people with strong opinions.

Therefore, the correct answer is "III only."

Other Forms of Sample Bias
?

Before we conclude, let's also note some other sources of bias that can occur during the sampling process.

  • Snowball Sampling

    This is where participants in a survey are asked to refer others, leading to an overrepresentation of certain social circles and the creation of clusters with similar characteristics.

  • Quota Sampling Without Randomization

    Filling demographic quotas (e.g., male, female) can result in individuals being selected haphazardly (not randomly) within each group.
