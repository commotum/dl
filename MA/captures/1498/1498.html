The following data set shows the total time, in hours, that a group of students spent studying for a final exam:

In a previous lesson, we saw that we can represent this data using a frequency table.

Number of Hours Studied Frequency

We interpret the data in this table as follows:

  • student spent zero hours studying

  • students spent one hour studying

  • student spent two hours studying

  • students spent three hours studying

This is an example of a one-way frequency table because it shows the relationship between a single variable (the number of hours studied) and the frequency of each value of the variable.

In this lesson, we'll learn how to represent data with two categorical variables.

Two-Way Frequency Tables
?

Suppose a gym surveys all of its members. They find that males and females exercise twice a week, and males and females exercise three times a week. How can we represent this information using a frequency table?

Notice that we now have two categorical variables, namely

  • the gender of each member (male or female), and

  • how often they exercise per week (twice a week or three times a week).

Since there are two categorical variables, we can organize this information using a joint frequency table.

To construct a joint frequency table, we first label the rows and columns using the possible values of our two categorical variables, as follows:

Note the following:

  • The numbers and are called the joint frequencies because they each represent joint data from two categorical variables.

  • Joint frequency tables are sometimes called two-way frequency tables or contingency tables.

For a given joint frequency table:

  • the sum of the joint frequencies in any row/column equals the marginal frequency for that row/column, and

  • the sum of the marginal frequencies in the "Totals" row/column equals the total
