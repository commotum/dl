Introduction

When researchers collect data, they usually take a sample from a larger population. From this sample, they calculate a number such as an average (mean) or a percentage (proportion). Because the result comes from only part of the population, it is not exact but an estimate of the true population value.

That is why statistical reports often include a parameter called the margin of error, which tells us how far the sample result might be from the actual population value.

To interpret a margin of error, we form a range:

This is called the plausible interval. It means that while we cannot know the exact population parameter, we can say that the true value is very likely (but not certain) to fall somewhere inside this interval.

So, the key ideas are the following:

  • The sample estimate is our best guess of a population parameter (like the mean or proportion) based on the data.

  • The margin of error adds "wiggle room" in both directions from the sample estimate.

  • Together, they create a plausible interval for the population parameter.

Interpreting the Margin of Error
?

Recall that the plausible interval created by the sample estimate and margin of error gives us a range for the corresponding population parameter (e.g., the mean or proportion).

It is important to understand what this interval does and does not mean.

  • What it DOES mean. The interval describes plausible values for the population parameter. The population value is very likely to lie within this interval, but not guaranteed. It is unlikely to lie outside the interval, but it is still possible.

  • What it DOES NOT mean. The interval does not describe individual values. Some (or even all) individuals in the population may fall outside the bounds of the interval.

    For example, suppose in a survey about students' study time, the plausible interval for the mean is hours per week. But this doesn't mean that all students study between and hours. Some might study less than hours or more than hours per week.

    In fact, roughly half of the students could study about hours and the other half about hours. Their mean would still be near hours, which lies between and .

Sample Size and Margin of Error
?

When you take a sample, the margin of error indicates how far your estimate might be from the true population value. If you only collect a small sample, random chance plays a larger role—one unusual data point can significantly shift your results. But as your sample size grows, those random ups and downs start to balance out. Each new data point adds a little more information and reduces the influence of any single outlier.

With larger samples, your estimate becomes more stable and precise, which means the margin of error decreases. In other words, the more people (or items) you include in your sample, the more confident you can be that your sample’s results are close to the actual truth for the whole population.

The rule of thumb is:

  • Smaller samples typically have larger margins of error.

  • Larger samples typically have smaller margins of error.

Let's see an example.

Example: The Relationship Between Margin of Error and Sample Size
?

A hospital studied a random sample of patients and found that the average waiting time in the emergency department was minutes, with a margin of error of minutes. The study was then repeated with a much larger sample size, and the new sample’s mean and margin of error were calculated in the same way as in the original study.

Which of the following statements is most likely true?

  1. The margin of error from the new study is smaller than the margin of error from the original study.
  2. The mean from the new study is larger than the mean from the original study.
  3. The mean from the new study is minutes.
EXPLANATION

More information is gathered in larger samples, which reduces the amount of random fluctuation in the sample estimate. As a result, the estimate is more stable and precise, with a smaller margin of error.

In our case, the new study has a much larger sample size than the original study. So, the margin of error from the new study is smaller than that from the original study. Therefore, statement I is likely true.

On the other hand, the new sample mean could be higher, lower, or essentially the same as the original mean; the direction is not determined by sample size alone. Therefore, statements II and III might not be true.

In conclusion, the correct answer is "I only."
