Intuition Behind the Implication Truth Table

Let's recall the truth table for

We consider a concrete example to build further intuition behind the truth values in this table.

Suppose your teacher promises that if you score at least on the exam, then you will receive an A. In other words, the teacher has promised that a score of at least on the exam implies that you will receive an A:

Now, the teacher may or may not be lying. The implication is true if the teacher tells the truth and false if the teacher lies.

The following four situations are possible:

  1. You score and receive an A. In this case, the teacher has told the truth. They have kept the promise.

  2. You score and do not receive an A. In this case, the teacher has lied. They have broken the promise.

  3. You do not score yet you receive an A. In this case, the teacher has told the truth! The promise does not apply because you did not score at least

  4. You do not score and do not receive an A. Again, the teacher has told the truth. The promise does not apply because you did not score at least

Example: Identifying True Conditional Statements
?

Which of the following statements are true?

  1. If is an even number, then is an odd number.
  2. If is an even number, then is an even number.
  3. If is an odd number, then is an odd number.
EXPLANATION

An implication is true unless is true and is false. In other words, the implication is true unless the "promise" is broken.

  • Statement I is true. The antecedent " is an even number" is true, and the consequent " is an odd number" is true. So the promise is kept, and the statement is true.

  • Statement II is false. The antecedent " is an even number" is true, and the consequent " is an even number" is false. So the promise is broken, and the statement is false.

  • Statement III is true. The antecedent " is an odd number" is false, so the promise does not apply. Regardless of the consequent, the promise is not broken, and the statement is true.

Therefore, the correct answer is "I and III only."
