A Word on Convergence
?

Term-by-term differentiation is a huge time-saver: it lets us obtain a Fourier series for without recomputing Fourier coefficients from scratch.

But differentiation also tends to make Fourier series "less convergent", it "spends" one power of . So, we need to be careful about what the differentiated series actually does.

In a -periodic Fourier series, differentiating introduces a factor of . So, very roughly, That means one level of coefficient decay is "used up" when we differentiate.

  • For the discontinuous but piecewise smooth sawtooth wave:

    • The function's jump discontinuities "use up" the smoothness budget. The coefficients decay only at a rate of

    • Differentiation cancels out the decay entirely: Since the terms of the series do not approach the series fails the basic divergence test and does not converge.

  1. The expression for the antiderivative is not a Fourier series in the usual sense because it contains the linear term

Note: Integration is generally "safer" than differentiation for Fourier series. Differentiation can slow convergence or even destroy it, whereas integration divides the th coefficient by which typically improves convergence.
