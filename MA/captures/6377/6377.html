Stability, Asymptotic Stability, and Instability
?

So far, we have learned how to find equilibrium points of a system However, just knowing where equilibrium points are is not enough: we also want to understand how nearby solutions behave when we start close to an equilibrium point.

Roughly speaking, there are three qualitatively different behaviors.

  • An equilibrium point is called asymptotically stable if there exists a neighborhood of such that every orbit that begins inside approaches as

    In plain words, this means that solutions that start sufficiently close to the equilibrium remain close and converge to the equilibrium as

  • An equilibrium point is called stable if for any neighborhood of there exists a neighborhood such that every orbit that begins inside at some point in time remains inside for all

    In plain words, this means that solutions that start sufficiently close to the equilibrium remain close, but may not converge to the equilibrium.

  • An equilibrium point is called unstable if it is not stable.

    In plain words, this means that solutions eventually move away from the equilibrium for some initial conditions arbitrarily close to it.

Watch out! These descriptions are local: they talk about what happens when the initial condition is sufficiently close to the equilibrium. They do not require that all trajectories in the entire plane behave the same way.

Next, let's see some examples.
