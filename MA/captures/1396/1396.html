Introduction
?

A population is a collection of items or people. For example, the students at a school, the supermarkets in a certain country, or the flowers in a park are all populations.

We often wish to gather information about a population. One way to do this is to conduct a census. A census is an investigation that considers every member of the population.

For example, an investigation where we measure the height of all the willow trees in England is a census.

There are some advantages to conducting a census:

  • they take every member of the population into consideration

  • they provide accurate answers

  • censuses are unbiased (more on this later)

However, populations are often large, so conducting a census can be impractical. In particular:

  • they are costly

  • they take a long time

  • it's difficult to know for sure that the entire population has been considered

For example, measuring the height of every willow tree in England would be expensive and challenging!

Sampling
?

It is common to conduct a sample survey to get around issues related to conducting censuses. With a survey, we gather information from a small subset of the population.

For example, rather than measuring the height of every willow tree in England, we could measure the height of all the willow trees in one of England's parks.

There are some advantages to conducting a sample:

  • Provided that a sample is representative of the corresponding population, we can use the information gathered from the sample to draw conclusions about the population as a whole.

  • Samples are cheaper and faster to conduct than censuses.

  • Samples are helpful when conducting an investigation that results in the destruction of the quantities being sampled. For example, using a sample to test the lifespan of a particular battery brand would require us to use up only a relatively small number of batteries from the population.

  • It is typically easier to access all the data needed for a sample.

While samples provide many benefits, there are also some disadvantages:

  • There is uncertainty from the fact that there is natural variation between different samples.

  • If the sample is biased, it will not truly represent the population. We'll discuss bias in more detail a little later.

When conducting a survey, we need to be sure that we're only considering a subset of the population of interest.

Example: Identifying Samples
?

Which of the following are samples from the population of high school teachers?

  1. All teachers of a particular high school aged years or more.
  2. A random selection of high school teachers with a master's degree.
  3. A random selection of people entering a particular high school's cafeteria one morning.
EXPLANATION

A sample is a subset of the population. Here, the population consists of all high school teachers.

  • I is a sample of high school teachers. The teachers in a particular high school aged years or more are all high school teachers.

  • II is a sample of high school teachers. Any high school teacher with a master's degree is a high school teacher.

  • III is not a sample of high school teachers. The people entering a high school cafeteria in the morning are not just teachers. They could also be students.

Therefore, the correct answer is "I and II only."

Example: Identifying Advantages and Disadvantages of Censuses and Samples
?

Suppose that we wish to study the population of residents of a certain building. Which is an advantage of using a census (instead of a sample survey)?

EXPLANATION

Censuses involve gathering information about every member of the population. In contrast, sample surveys involve collecting information from a subset of the population and using the information to draw inferences about the population.

In our case, a census will require collecting information from all residents, while a sample survey would require collecting information from a subset of residents. So, the information gathered from the census will be more thorough than a sample survey.

Sampling Units, Sampling Frames, and Bias
?

There is specific terminology that we use when discussing samples from a population.

  • The individual units of the population under consideration are called sampling units.

  • Sampling units are ordered in a list called a sampling frame. The sampling frame contains all sampling units in a population and is used to create a sample.

For example, suppose we were collecting data about the ages of students in a high school. In that case, the sampling units are the individual students, and the sampling frame might be a list containing the details of every student in a school.

When selecting a sample from a population, we must be careful not to introduce bias into the sample. Bias is any way the sample fails to represent the population from which it was drawn.

One way to avoid bias is through random sampling. In random sampling, each sampling unit is chosen randomly from the sampling frame so that every sampling unit has an equal chance of being selected.

A sample is representative of the population if it is not biased.

Example: Identifying Sources of Bias
?

Suppose we wish to study the population of shoppers that visit a mall. If we sample women with children visiting the mall, then which of the following are sources of bias in our sample?

  1. All the sampling units are women.
  2. All the sampling units have children.
  3. All the sampling units are visiting the mall.
EXPLANATION

Bias is any way the sample fails to represent the population from which it was drawn.

Here, the population consists of all shoppers that visit a certain mall.

  • I is a source of bias. All the sampling units are women, but not all mall visitors are women.

  • II is a source of bias. All the sampling units have children, but other visitors to the mall may not have children.

  • III is not a source of bias. All the sampling units are visitors to the mall, the population we're considering.

Therefore, the correct answer is "I and II only."

Example: Classifying Samples as Representative or Biased
?

Suppose that we wish to study the population of products in a supermarket. Which of the following samples would be representative of the population?

  1. A selection of products that have a discount for promotion.
  2. A selection of cleaning and personal hygiene products.
  3. A selection of products randomly chosen from the supermarket inventory.
EXPLANATION

A bias is any way the sample fails to represent the population from which it was drawn.

A sample is representative of the population if it is not biased.

Here, the population consists of all products in a supermarket.

  • Sample I is biased. All the sampling units are products that have a discount for promotion. However, most products in the supermarket do not have discounts for promotion.

  • Sample II is biased. All the sampling units are cleaning and personal hygiene products, but most products in a typical supermarket are not for cleaning and personal hygiene.

  • Sample III is not biased. All the products in the supermarket are listed in the supermarket inventory and therefore have an equal chance of being chosen.

Therefore, the correct answer is "III only."
