The Significance of Bayes' Theorem

For reasons that will become clear shortly, we'll rewrite Bayes' theorem in a slightly different (but equivalent) way, as follows:

Bayes' theorem is significant because it allows us to update our belief that a particular hypothesis is true when new evidence comes to light. For this reason, it has numerous applications in science, computer science, and engineering.

For example, suppose a doctor wishes to assess the probability that a certain patient is infected with a particular disease. It is known that those aged or older have a higher chance of carrying the disease.

Let's define the events and as follows:

  • the event that a randomly selected patient has the disease

Let's introduce some new terminology:

  • is called the prior. It tells us the probability that a randomly selected patient has the disease without considering any other factors.

  • is called the posterior. It tells us the probability that a randomly selected patient has the disease taking into consideration that they are or older.

  • is called the likelihood. It tells us the proportion of infected people that are or older. This quantity could be established by taking a random sample from the population of infected people and computing the ratio of those that are or older.

Therefore, Bayes' theorem allows us to update the hypothesis (the patient has the disease), taking into account information about the patient (they are or older), making use of existing available evidence (an estimate of the proportion of infected people that are or older).

Furthermore, suppose the likelihood is updated at a later date (e.g., due to more evidence being available as more people become infected). In that case, this will further improve the accuracy of the posterior when future patients are assessed.
