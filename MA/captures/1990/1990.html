Final Remarks
?

Throughout this lesson, we've defined the double integral of over as the unique number (if it exists) that satisfies the inequality for all partitions of and we write

We note the following:

  • Up to now, we've assumed that is continuous and is closed and bounded. Whenever this is the case, a unique number will always exist.

  • For positive, continuous functions, the double integral gives the exact volume bounded between the surface and

  • More generally, the double integral gives a signed volume. If is negative everywhere on then the double integral of over is negative. If is both positive and negative on then the double integral gives the difference between the positive and negative unsigned volumes. This is similar to the case with definite integrals for single-valued functions.

  • Finally, It's possible to use this definition with functions that are not continuous. If a function (not necessarily continuous) is defined over we say that is Riemann integrable over if a unique number can be found that satisfies the above inequality. Many (though not all) non-continuous functions are Riemann integrable.
