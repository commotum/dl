In a relationship between two variables and a line of best fit (or trend line) captures the general direction of the data, even if no single data point lies exactly on it.

But how should we interpret the line in relation to the actual data points?

We do so using the line's predictions. A data point's predicted -value is the -value of the point on the line of best fit at the same -coordinate as the point.

Consider the scatter plot below, which shows data points in the relationship between two variables and along with a line of best fit.

The line of best fit provides an overall model of the data, but it is not exact for every individual point:

  • A data point's actual -value is less than the -value predicted by the line of best fit if it lies below this line.

    Since the predicted -value is greater than the actual -value (), the line of best fit overestimates these data points. In this case, there is a total of data points below the line.

  • On the other hand, a data point's actual -value is greater than the -value predicted by the line of best fit if it lies above this line.

    Since the predicted -value is less than the actual -value (), the line of best fit underestimates these data points. In this case, there is a total of data points above the line.
